The Tor Project is the nonprofit organization that develops and maintains the Tor (The Onion Router) anonymity network, which routes internet traffic through a series of encrypted relays to obscure users' identities and locations. Originally developed by the US Naval Research Laboratory, Tor was spun out into a nonprofit in 2006, and the network has become the primary technical infrastructure for internet anonymity available to at-risk users globally. Schneier serves on the Tor Project's board of directors.

## Tor as Security Infrastructure

Tor's technical design directly embodies Schneier's analysis in [[data-and-goliath]] and [[click-here-to-kill-everybody]]: in a world where metadata collection is pervasive and network-level surveillance is industrial-scale, anonymizing the transport layer is a necessary (though not sufficient) component of privacy protection. Tor addresses the structural asymmetry between powerful surveillance actors — state intelligence agencies, commercial data brokers, repressive governments — and individuals whose threat models require communication that cannot be traced back to them.

The [[snowden-revelations]] confirmed that NSA documents specifically discussed the difficulty of defeating Tor at scale, validating the network's practical security properties against the most sophisticated known adversary. This confirmation aligned with Schneier's consistent argument that cryptographic and anonymization tools that are mathematically sound provide real protection even against nation-state adversaries — one of the core claims of [[applied-cryptography]] extended to the anonymization domain.

## Board Membership and Alignment

Schneier's board membership connects his analytical framework to the Tor Project's governance and strategic decisions. The [[security-mindset]] is essential for evaluating proposals that would modify Tor's design in response to policy pressure — governments periodically argue that anonymity tools must be backdoored or weakened for law enforcement purposes, exactly the argument that Schneier analyzed as technically incoherent in the [[crypto-wars-export-controls]] era. His presence on the board provides institutional memory of why those arguments failed then and why they remain technically invalid.

The Tor Project also sits at the intersection of the [[electronic-frontier-foundation]] network — EFF helped incubate Tor's nonprofit transition — and the broader ecosystem of digital rights organizations including [[accessnow]] where Schneier is also a board member. These overlapping board memberships reflect the coherence of Schneier's institutional commitments: anonymity tools, digital rights advocacy, and surveillance accountability form a unified response to the power-asymmetry problem that his books have analyzed across three decades.
