Tim O'Reilly (born 1954) played a pivotal role in making open source legible and palatable to the technology business world, primarily through publishing, conference-organizing, and strategic communication.

O'Reilly Media had been publishing technical books on Unix tools and the emerging internet infrastructure throughout the 1990s — books that primarily documented open source software (Perl, Python, Linux, Apache) long before the term "open source" existed. When the term was coined in February 1998 (see [[foresight-open-source-meeting-1998]]), O'Reilly organized the Open Source Summit in April 1998 — the first major gathering under the new label, which brought together Torvalds, Stallman, [[eric-raymond]], [[bruce-perens]], [[larry-wall]], [[guido-van-rossum]], and others. The summit helped consolidate the open source identity and introduced key figures to business and press audiences.

O'Reilly launched OSCON (the O'Reilly Open Source Convention) in 1999, which became the flagship annual event for the open source movement through the 2010s, providing a commercial venue where FOSS developers, corporate adopters, and the technology press intersected.

The [[open-sources-anthology-1999]], co-edited by O'Reilly, assembled essays by Torvalds, Raymond, Perens, [[christine-peterson]], [[guido-van-rossum]], and others into the movement's first collective intellectual statement aimed at a business audience.

O'Reilly's framing of technology trends — he later coined "Web 2.0" and "Government as a Platform" — tended toward pragmatic embrace rather than ideological clarity. His relationship with the free software tradition was always arms-length: he published [[cathedral-and-the-bazaar-1997]] as a book and gave Raymond a major platform, but was not a participant in the philosophical debates between [[software-freedom-vs-open-source]]. This pragmatism made him effective as a movement evangelist but sometimes frustrating to movement purists.
