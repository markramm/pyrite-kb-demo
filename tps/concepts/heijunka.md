Production leveling — smoothing the volume and mix of production over time to eliminate the peaks and valleys that create [[muri-and-mura|overburden (muri) and unevenness (mura)]]. Instead of producing large batches of one model followed by large batches of another, heijunka distributes production of all models evenly throughout the day. This requires the fast changeovers that [[smed]] enables and the flexible workforce that [[standard-work]] trains. Heijunka is the least intuitive TPS concept for mass-production thinkers — it appears to sacrifice economies of scale. But [[taiichi-ohno]] argued that the apparent efficiency of large batches is illusory: they create inventory (waste), amplify demand variation upstream (the bullwhip effect), and hide quality problems. Leveled production creates stable demand on every upstream process, making [[just-in-time]] and [[kanban]] possible. The heijunka box (a visual scheduling device) makes the production plan visible and is one of TPS's signature visual management tools. Heijunka was systematized during the [[tps-maturation-1965-1980]] era.
