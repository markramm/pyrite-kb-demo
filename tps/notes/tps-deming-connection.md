The Deming-TPS connection is foundational but often oversimplified. [[w-edwards-deming]] taught statistical quality control and PDCA to Japanese industry through [[juse]] in 1950. [[taiichi-ohno]] built on this foundation but went far beyond it. Key transmission points: (1) PDCA became the rhythm of [[kaizen]] — every improvement follows Plan-Do-Check-Act, (2) statistical thinking about variation informed [[standard-work]] and process stability, (3) Deming's insistence that quality is management's responsibility (not workers') is embedded in TPS culture. But Ohno added what Deming did not: flow thinking ([[one-piece-flow]], [[pull-production]]), waste taxonomy ([[seven-wastes-muda]]), and the physical production system ([[kanban]], [[jidoka]], [[smed]]). Deming provided the intellectual soil; Ohno grew something Deming never imagined. The [[toyota-wins-deming-prize-1965|Deming Prize win in 1965]] symbolizes both the connection and the transcendence — Toyota earned the prize by applying Deming's methods, but the system they built had already moved beyond Deming's framework.
