Creative Commons is a nonprofit organization that provides free, standardized copyright licenses enabling creators to legally share their work. Founded by [[lawrence-lessig]] in 2001 and launched formally at [[creative-commons-launch-2001]], CC licenses have become the backbone of the open content ecosystem, used by Wikipedia, Flickr, and millions of creators worldwide.

Doctorow was among the earliest and most prominent adopters of Creative Commons licensing, releasing all of his novels under CC licenses from the start of his fiction career. This practice — giving away free digital copies while selling print editions — became a model he championed as proof that [[creative-commons-licensing]] and commercial success are complementary rather than opposed. His novels published through [[tor-books]] were among the first from a major publisher to carry CC licenses.

The CC framework embodies several principles central to Doctorow's worldview: that [[switching-costs]] and artificial scarcity harm culture; that the [[end-to-end-principle]] should apply to cultural production; and that empowering readers and remixers ultimately strengthens rather than weakens creators. His nonfiction, including [[information-doesnt-want-to-be-free]], argues extensively for this position.

Creative Commons represents an early, successful instance of [[self-help-ip]] — using the existing intellectual property system's tools to carve out space for sharing rather than waiting for legislative reform.
