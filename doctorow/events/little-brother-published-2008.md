In April 2008, [[tor-books]] published [[little-brother]], Doctorow's young adult novel about a teenage hacker who fights back against post-9/11 surveillance overreach in San Francisco. The publication was a turning point in Doctorow's career and influence: the novel reached far beyond the technology and science fiction communities that had been his primary audiences and introduced his ideas to teenagers, teachers, librarians, and general readers worldwide.

[[little-brother]] is set in a near-future San Francisco where the Department of Homeland Security has effectively occupied the city after a terrorist attack. The protagonist, Marcus Yallow (aka w1n5t0n), uses his technical knowledge of [[adversarial-interoperability]], cryptography, and network security to organize resistance to surveillance infrastructure. The novel is simultaneously a thriller, a civics lesson, and a how-to guide for thinking about [[digital-rights-management-critique]] and security culture.

The book won the Prometheus Award for libertarian science fiction and was widely adopted in schools and libraries. It was distributed free under a [[creative-commons-licensing]] license alongside its print publication, continuing the practice Doctorow had established with [[down-and-out-in-the-magic-kingdom]]. The free digital distribution drove rather than cannibalized print sales, strengthening the argument he made throughout the [[eff-and-fiction-era-2007-2015]].

[[edward-snowden]]'s 2013 revelations about NSA surveillance retroactively confirmed the novel's premises in ways that gave it a second wave of attention. The sequel [[homeland]] (2013) extended Marcus's story into the Occupy era. Together, the two novels form a bildungsroman of political awakening through technical understanding — the kind of reader Doctorow was trying to cultivate throughout his career.
