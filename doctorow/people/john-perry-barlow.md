John Perry Barlow (1947–2018) was a Wyoming cattle rancher, lyricist for the Grateful Dead, and co-founder of the [[electronic-frontier-foundation]]. His 1996 "Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace" is one of the most read and debated documents in internet history — a utopian proclamation of digital sovereignty that has aged into a complex symbol of what early internet idealism got right and wrong.

## The Declaration of Independence of Cyberspace

Written at the World Economic Forum in Davos during the passage of the Communications Decency Act, the Declaration announced that cyberspace was a sovereign realm beyond government jurisdiction: "Governments of the Industrial World, you weary giants of flesh and steel, I come from Cyberspace, the new home of Mind... You have no moral right to rule us nor do you possess any methods of enforcement we have true reason to fear." It became the canonical text of cyber-libertarianism — the view that the internet's decentralized architecture was inherently liberatory and would route around attempts at state control.

Doctorow's relationship to this tradition is complex. He works within the EFF, which Barlow co-founded, and shares Barlow's commitment to digital rights. But the [[enshittification]] framework represents a direct critique of naive cyber-libertarianism: platforms use their technical architecture not to route around control but to impose it. The Declaration's confidence that the internet's structure would defeat corporate and state power looks different after two decades of platform monopolization. Doctorow has engaged with Barlow's legacy in [[pluralistic-blog]] with both respect and critical distance — honoring the founding impulse while rejecting the conclusion that technical architecture was sufficient guarantee of freedom.

## EFF and early digital rights

Barlow co-founded the [[electronic-frontier-foundation]] in 1990 with Mitch Kapor and John Gilmore after the Secret Service's raid on Steve Jackson Games revealed the legal vulnerability of online speech and data. The EFF's founding mission — to bring civil liberties principles to cyberspace — created the organizational infrastructure that Doctorow would later work within as European Director. Barlow's contribution was ideological and rhetorical: he articulated why digital rights mattered in terms that resonated well beyond technical communities.

## Cyber-libertarianism and its limits

Barlow's politics were explicitly libertarian — he distrusted both government and corporate power but believed technical architecture (encryption, decentralization, open protocols) was the appropriate remedy. He did not develop a systematic critique of monopoly power or platform concentration; that critique came later, from people like [[tim-wu]], [[lina-khan]], and Doctorow himself. Barlow's Declaration assumes that the internet's architecture would prevent the consolidation of power that [[chokepoint-capitalism]] describes. The fact that it did not is partly a technical story (the [[end-to-end-principle]] was not enforced against platform layer concentration) and partly a political one.

Doctorow's fiction from [[little-brother]] onward depicts protagonists who are Barlowian in spirit — committed to technical self-determination and civil liberties — but who face corporate and state power that technical cleverness alone cannot defeat. The tension between Barlow's optimism and Doctorow's more structural analysis is a productive thread through Doctorow's work.
