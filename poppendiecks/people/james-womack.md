James Womack is the researcher and author who, with [[daniel-jones]], codified [[taiichi-ohno]]'s Toyota Production System for Western audiences under the name "Lean." His two major books — "The Machine That Changed the World" (1990) and "Lean Thinking" (1996) — gave the Poppendiecks their primary conceptual vocabulary and the value stream framework they explicitly adopted when translating lean principles into software.

## Naming lean

Womack's decision to call TPS-derived production methods "Lean" was not merely terminological. The name concentrated a set of ideas — minimize waste, maximize flow, pull rather than push, continuous improvement — into a label that practitioners could attach to their work. When [[mary-poppendieck]] and [[tom-poppendieck]] titled their first book "Lean Software Development," they were deliberately positioning it within Womack's lean tradition rather than, say, the Agile movement or quality management traditions separately.

## Value stream thinking

"Lean Thinking" introduced value stream mapping — tracing the sequence of steps that deliver value to the customer and identifying waste at each step — as a core lean tool. The Poppendiecks adapted this directly as [[value-stream-mapping-for-software]], applying the same logic to software delivery pipelines: map the flow from customer request to delivered software, find the delays and handoffs, and eliminate them.

## Five lean principles

Womack and Jones articulated five lean principles in "Lean Thinking": specify value, identify the value stream, flow, pull, and pursue perfection. The Poppendiecks' [[seven-lean-principles]] extend and reframe this list for the software context — retaining the core logic while adding principles like [[amplify-learning]] and [[decide-as-late-as-possible]] that reflect software development's distinctive nature as a knowledge work and learning process.

## Lean Enterprise Institute

Womack founded the Lean Enterprise Institute to extend lean thinking beyond automotive manufacturing into other industries and service sectors. This institutional work helped create the broader lean community in which the Poppendiecks' software application found receptive audiences.
