John Gilmore was the fifth employee of Sun Microsystems and later became one of the most prominent figures at the intersection of free software and civil liberties. His connections to [[stallman]] and the [[gnu-project]] run across multiple institutions.

As a programmer, Gilmore contributed to GNU tools including GNU tar and work on debugger infrastructure. More consequentially, he co-founded Cygnus Solutions in 1989 with Michael Tiemann and David Henkel-Wallace — the first company built around the business model of selling support for free software, a practical demonstration that the [[free-software-definition]] was compatible with commercial viability.

In 1990, Gilmore co-founded the [[electronic-frontier-foundation]] with Mitch Kapor and [[john-gilmore|himself]] in response to a series of government raids on hackers. The EFF became the primary civil liberties organization defending digital rights in the courts and legislatures, operating in an adjacent space to the [[free-software-foundation]]'s software-focused mission. The two organizations have frequently aligned on issues of encryption, surveillance, and the [[right-to-read]] type of threats that [[stallman]] analyzed.

Gilmore has served on the FSF board, including in 2024, representing continuity of the original hacker-culture cohort that surrounded [[stallman]] in the [[mit-ai-lab-hacker-culture-1971-1984]] era and its aftermath. He was present at the [[netscape-mozilla-open-source-1998]] strategy session where [[christine-peterson]] coined "open source," though Gilmore's own commitments remained closer to Stallman's freedom-centered framing.

His broader activism — including litigation over the right to travel without ID and work on cryptographic freedom — reflects the same instinct that produced the [[four-freedoms]]: that individuals' control over their tools and communications is a matter of fundamental liberty, not mere convenience.
