Richard Stallman (born 1953) is the central figure of this knowledge base — programmer, activist, and originator of the [[free-software-definition]] and the [[copyleft]] concept. He launched the [[gnu-project]] in 1983 with the [[announcement-of-gnu-project]], and founded the [[free-software-foundation]] in 1985.

Stallman was shaped by the hacker culture of the [[mit-ai-lab]] during the era of [[mit-ai-lab-hacker-culture-1971-1984]], where software was freely shared among researchers. His turn toward activism came when proprietary software began displacing that culture. His response was the [[gnu-manifesto]] and the legal instrument of [[gpl-copyleft-mechanism]].

His articulation of the [[four-freedoms]] — to run, study, redistribute, and improve software — defines the philosophical core of the movement he founded. He insists on the distinction captured in [[libre-vs-gratis]]: "free as in freedom," not merely free of charge.

The [[gpl-v1]], [[gpl-v2]], and ultimately [[gpl-v3]] are his primary legal instruments. The GPLv3 addressed [[tivoization]] and [[digital-restrictions-management]], but [[linus-torvalds]] refused to adopt it for Linux, illustrating the tension documented in [[software-freedom-vs-open-source]].

Stallman has written extensively, including [[why-software-should-be-free]], [[right-to-read-essay]], [[can-you-trust-your-computer]], [[who-does-that-server-really-serve]], and [[why-schools-should-use-free-software]]. His collected essays appear in [[free-software-free-society]]. His most prominent public talk was [[tedx-geneva-2014]], delivered in 2014 as a comprehensive statement on free software and free society.

His biography was written by [[sam-williams]] as [[free-as-in-freedom-williams]], later revised by Stallman himself as [[free-as-in-freedom-revised]]. The [[hacker-ethic-mit]] that shaped him is documented in [[hackers-levy]] by [[steven-levy]].

Legal work extending his ideas was done by [[eben-moglen]] and [[larry-lessig]]. [[bradley-kuhn]] is his principal protégé in GPL enforcement. The schism with [[eric-raymond]], [[bruce-perens]], and the [[open-source-initiative]] is the subject of [[why-open-source-misses-the-point]] and the note [[stallman-vs-open-source-philosophical-core]].

The [[free-software-foundation]] was co-founded by [[stallman]] with [[harold-abelson]], [[robert-chassell]], [[gerald-sussman]], and [[leonard-tower-jr]] in 1985. The [[fsf-board-controversy-2019]] resulted in Stallman's resignation from the FSF board; his [[stallman-2021-return-fsf|return in 2021]] triggered an open letter signed by over 3,000 people calling for his removal, alongside a counter-letter of over 2,000 supporters.
