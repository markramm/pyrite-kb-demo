The Open Source Initiative (OSI) was founded in 1998 by [[eric-raymond]] and [[bruce-perens]] in the wake of Netscape's announcement that it would release the source code of its browser. The founding is a key event in [[open-source-definition-schism]], part of the [[free-vs-open-source-schism-1998-2007]] era.

The OSI was established to promote a deliberately pragmatic, business-friendly reframing of [[stallman]]'s [[free-software-definition]]. The "Open Source Definition" — derived from [[bruce-perens]]'s Debian Free Software Guidelines — covers much of the same technical ground as the [[free-software-definition]] but strips out Stallman's explicit ethical framing around [[software-freedom-vs-open-source]].

[[stallman]] regards the OSI's framing as evasive: the term "open source" focuses on practical benefits (better code, security, collaboration) while avoiding the ethical argument that proprietary software wrongs users by denying them the [[four-freedoms]]. His critique is laid out in [[why-open-source-misses-the-point]] and documented in [[stallman-vs-open-source-philosophical-core]].

The OSI approves licenses as "open source," including many that do not include [[copyleft]] provisions. This is significant because non-copyleft "open source" licenses permit companies to take open-source code and make proprietary products from it — exactly what [[stallman]]'s [[gpl-copyleft-mechanism]] was designed to prevent.

[[tim-oreilly]] and [[linus-torvalds]] have generally aligned with the OSI's framing. [[bradley-kuhn]] and [[eben-moglen]] have maintained Stallman's [[free-software-definition]] standard. The tension between these camps continues to shape software licensing debates.
