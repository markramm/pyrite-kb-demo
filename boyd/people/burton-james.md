## Overview

Colonel James G. Burton was a U.S. Army officer and one of Boyd's most prominent acolytes, known for his fight to impose honest live-fire testing on weapons systems — most famously the Bradley Fighting Vehicle. His experience is the most dramatic public illustration of what Boyd's "To Be or To Do" choice looks like in practice.

## The Bradley Fighting Vehicle Battle

In the early 1980s, Burton was assigned to the Office of the Secretary of Defense to oversee live-fire testing of the Bradley Infantry Fighting Vehicle. The Bradley was designed to carry infantry into combat while providing fire support — but its design had been compromised by decades of requirements creep, turning a simple armored personnel carrier into an overloaded, under-armored hybrid.

Burton insisted on realistic testing: shooting the Bradley with the weapons it would actually face in combat, with fuel and ammunition aboard, to see if the vehicle would protect the soldiers inside. The Army and defense contractors resisted fiercely, because realistic testing would reveal that the Bradley's aluminum armor could not withstand the threats it was designed to face.

The battle lasted years. Burton was reassigned, marginalized, and pressured to accept watered-down test protocols. He persisted, ultimately forcing tests that revealed serious survivability problems. The resulting modifications — additional armor, relocated fuel cells, improved fire suppression — significantly improved the vehicle's survivability. When Bradleys saw combat in the 1991 Gulf War, the modifications Burton had fought for saved lives.

## The Pentagon Wars

Burton's book "The Pentagon Wars: Reformers Challenge the Old Guard" (1993) documented his experience in detail, exposing the institutional dynamics that prioritized contractor relationships and career management over soldier safety. The book was adapted into a 1998 HBO film starring Kelsey Grammer and Cary Elwes, bringing the military reform critique to a popular audience.

The film, while comedic in tone, captured the core Boyd insight: institutions dominated by "to be" culture will systematically resist evidence that threatens programs, budgets, and careers — even when soldiers' lives are at stake.

## Significance to Boyd's Legacy

Burton's story is important to the Boyd KB because it demonstrates the practical consequences of Boyd's theoretical framework:
- **Institutional orientation** — the Army's mental model prioritized the Bradley program's survival over honest assessment
- **To Be or To Do** — Burton chose accountability over career advancement, paying the price Boyd predicted
- **Moral vs. physical** — the fight was not about engineering but about institutional honesty
- **The reform network** — Burton operated within Boyd's network of reformers (including Thomas Christie at DOT&E), showing how the network translated ideas into institutional action
