## Content

This piece explores the mysterious disappearance of Philip Agre, a pioneering computer scientist and UCLA professor who predicted -- three decades ago -- how artificial intelligence would fundamentally transform privacy, behavior manipulation, and the distinction between information and misinformation.

### Agre's Background and Insight

Agre possessed a rare combination of expertise: he understood computer systems from a technical perspective while also grasping their philosophical and human implications. Rather than remaining confined to mathematical analysis, he studied philosophy on its own terms to better comprehend AI's broader significance.

### His Warnings

Agre recognized that artificial intelligence development was not philosophically neutral. He anticipated that AI research would systematically benefit powerful entities -- corporations and governments -- rather than serve broader human interests. His concerns have proven prescient regarding election interference and the developmental impacts of virtual environments on young people.

### The Disappearance

Despite the prescience of his work, Agre stepped away from public discourse nearly two decades ago. Friends reported him missing, and when colleagues attempted to archive his writings, he declined participation. His current whereabouts and activities remain unknown.

### The Author's Reflection

Lorinczi expresses poignancy about Agre's withdrawal, comparing his situation to a classical tragedy where seeing too much truth becomes psychologically unbearable.
