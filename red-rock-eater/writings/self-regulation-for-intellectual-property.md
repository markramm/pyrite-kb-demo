# self-regulation for intellectual property

```
[The enclosed text, which came in over the transom, is a parody of a
recent US Commerce Department statement on self-regulation of privacy
on the Internet.  The author(s) noticed a gaping disparity between the
administration's approach to the information rights of large organized
interests -- it supports criminalizing the mere possession of hardware
or software that is even theoretically capable of facilitating the
abuse of intellectual property -- and the information rights of
ordinary citizens -- abusers of personal information are asked pretty
please to be nice.  If the tables were turned, the result would be
amusing, as the parody demonstrates.  The drawn-out saga of privacy
self-regulation, however, has not been amusing.  We seem to go through
a three-stroke cycle: first the issue becomes a crisis, as if it had
never been noticed before; then grand promises are made about self-
regulation, and we are all asked to give these promises time to work;
then everyone forgets about the issue for a while; and finally the
cycle begins anew with nobody the wiser except of course the public,
whose strong support for a common-sense regulatory framework hardly
wavers.  With the rise of the Internet, this cycle seems to have sped
up; its most recent iteration has only taken about a year.  The whole
idea of self-regulation never made much sense; it presupposes that the
great majority of firms are motivated to join a trade association, and
that this trade association's staff members are motivated to overcome
their inherent conflict of interest by penalizing their own employers
by enforcing codes of conduct when privacy violations occur.  This
scenario was already implausible in the old economy with its dominance
by large, stable firms.  It is even less plausible in the new digital
economy, where new technology permits small firms to create impressive
Web storefronts for no capital and companies come and go at the flick
of a switch.  Even the term "fly-by-night company", which epitomizes
our notions of fraudulent trading, is a vestige of the old economy,
with its presupposition that large, static firms are the norm and that
small, flexible, rapidly changing companies are suspect.  It follows
that, quite the contrary of the hypnotic chants now emanating from the
former party of the people, self-regulation is an anachronism in the
new economy.  As the recent work of the FTC has demonstrated, direct
marketing on the Internet thus far has been a tornado of fraud, and
that pattern of fraudulent behavior has extended to the information-
collection practices of online firms -- to such an extent, incredibly,
that we have almost become accustomed to firms whose Web sites use
deception to lure children into surrendering personal information that
will be used for other purposes without the knowledge or permission
of the children's parents.  Participants in the industry still report
that lawyers warn companies against posting privacy policies on the
grounds that they would have to follow them.  This sorry fact betrays
a fundamental problem: that the great majority of online firms still
treat privacy as a public-relations problem.  The problem is handed
off to the PR people and the lobbyists, leaving the underlying traffic
in personal information untouched.  Privacy protection, however, is
not just a matter of posting anodyne statements of concern.  It is
a matter of changing practices, architectures, and other elements of
a business model.  It will be objected that companies that make these
changes will find their attention distracted just long enough to lose
a step in merciless winner-take-all markets.  But that is just the
point: the market is not functioning correctly here.  Consumers are
resisting the new online medium in droves because of privacy concerns,
to such an extent that the Internet's reputation threatens to sink
into the same swamp as 900 telephone numbers.  In short, the growth of
this promising medium and the industry that supports it are threatened
by a serious pattern of dysfunction.  It so happens that the European
privacy regulations are serving as a reality check here.  Those rules
are not new, however, and not the end of the world; American companies
have been successfully operating under them for decades.  The United
States helped pioneer the conceptual framework that eventually took
shape in those regulations, but we abandoned that path 25 years ago
and in so doing have created a tremendous mess that the resistance to
Internet commerce is bringing back forcefully to our attention.  Let's
just stop this futile dance of empty promises and just fix the problem
so we can get on with creating useful new electronic institutions.]

Piracy Parody

Elements of Effective Self-Regulation for Prevention of Piracy

[This parody is not meant to be disrespectful, but to point out the
analogy between privacy rights (which are relatively unprotected by
US law) and intellectual property rights (which are relatively well
protected)]

As set forth in "A Framework for Global Electronic Commerce", the
Clinton Administration supports the general public's efforts to
implement meaningful, company-friendly, self-regulatory regimes to
prevent piracy.  To be meaningful, self-regulation must do more than
articulate broad policies or guidelines.  Effective self-regulation
involves substantive rules, as well as the means to ensure that
companies know the rules, that consumers comply with them, and
that companies have appropriate recourse when injuries result
from noncompliance.  This paper discusses the elements of effective
self-regulatory regimes--one that incorporates principles of fair
use practices with enforcement mechanisms that assure compliance with
those practices.

A. Principles of Fair Use Practices

Fair use practices form the basis for the Copyright Act of 1974, the
legislation that prevents original works from being copied by the
United States government.  In 1980, these principles were adopted by
the World Intellectual Property Organization.

Principles of fair use practices include company awareness, choice,
appropriate levels of security, data integrity, and company access
to their registered creative works.  While the discussion that follows
suggests ways in which these principles can be implemented, the public
is encouraged to develop its own ways of accomplishing this goal.

1. Awareness.  At a minimum, companies need to know the distributors
of their copyright information, the intended uses of the information,
and the means by which they may limit its reproduction.  Consumers are
responsible for raising company awareness and can do so through the
following avenues:

* * Piracy policies.  Piracy policies articulate the manner in which

a consumer collects, uses, and reproduces copyright works, and the
choices they offer companies to exercise rights in their copyright
information.  On the basis of this policy, companies can determine
whether and to what extent they wish to make works available to
consumers.

* * Notification.  A consumer's piracy policy should be made known to

companies.  Notification should be written in language that is clear
and easily understood, should be displayed prominently, and should be
made available before companies are asked to provide copyright works
to the consumer.

* * Company education.  Consumers should teach companies to ask for

relevant knowledge about why works are being copied, what the copies
will be used for, how they will be protected, the consequences of
providing or withholding works, and any recourse they may have.
Company education enables companies to make informed decisions about
how they allow their copyright works to be used as they participate
in the information economy.  Company education may be carried out
by individual consumers, trade unions, or public service campaigns.

2. Choice.  Companies should be given the opportunity to exercise
choice with respect to whether and how their copyright works are used,
either by consumers with whom they have direct contact or by third
parties.  Companies must be provided with simple, readily visible,
available, and affordable mechanisms--whether through technological
means or otherwise--to exercise this option.  For certain kinds
of information, e.g., confidential corporate planning documents
or information related to takeover offers, affirmative choice by
companies may be appropriate.  In these cases consumers should not
reproduce the works unless its use is explicitly consented to by
a senior officer of the company or, in the case of a takeover, the
company making the offer.

3. Data Security.  Consumers creating, maintaining, using or
disseminating copyright works must take reasonable measures to
assure its suitability for its intended use and must take reasonable
precautions to protect it from loss, misuse, alteration or
destruction.  The consumer should also strive to assure that the level
of prevention extended by third parties to whom they transfer copies
is at a level comparable to his or her own.

4. Data Integrity.  Consumers should keep only copyright works
relevant for the purposes for which they were gathered, consistent
with the principles of awareness and choice.  To the extent necessary
for those purposes, the copies should be accurate, complete, and
current.

5. Company Access.  Companies should have the opportunity for
reasonable, appropriate access to information created by them that
a consumer holds, and be able to update those works when necessary.
The extent of access may vary from demographic to demographic.
Providing access to copyright works can be costly to consumers, and
thus decisions about the level of appropriate access should take into
account the nature of the copyright work, the number of locations in
which it is stored, the nature of the residence, and the ways in which
the information is to be used.

6. Accountability.  Consumers should be held accountable for complying
with their piracy policies.

B. Enforcement.

To be effective, a self-regulatory intellectual property regime
should include mechanisms to assure compliance with the rules and
appropriate recourse to an injured party when rules are not followed.
Such mechanisms are essential tools to enable companies to exercise
their intellectual property rights, and should, therefore, be readily
available and affordable to companies.  They may take a variety
of forms and businesses may need to use more than one depending
upon the personality of the consumer and the kind and sensitivity
of information the consumer copies and uses.  The discussion
of enforcement tools below is in no way intended to be limiting.
Consumer groups may design the means to provide enforcement that best
suit its needs and the needs of their members.

1. Company recourse.  Consumers who copy and use copyright works
should offer companies mechanisms by which their complaints and
disputes can be resolved.  Such mechanisms should be readily available
and affordable.

2. Verification.  Verification provides attestation that the
assertions consumers make about their piracy practices are true
and that piracy practices have been implemented as represented.
The nature and the extent of verification depends upon the kind of
information which a consumer copies--consumers using highly sensitive
information may be held to a higher standard of verification.  Because
verification may be costly for consumers, work needs to be done to
arrive at appropriate, cost-effective ways to provide companies with
the means to provide verification.

3. Consequences.  For self-regulation to be effective, failure to
comply with fair use practices should have consequences.  Examples
of such consequences include cancellation of the right to use a
certifying seal or logo, posting the name of the non-complier on a
"bad-actor" list, or disqualification from membership in a book club
or magazine subscription.  Non-compliers could be required to pay
the costs of determining their non-compliance.  Ultimately, sanctions
should be stiff enough to be meaningful and swift enough to assure
companies that their concerns are addressed in a timely fashion.
When consumers make assertions that they are abiding by certain piracy
practices and then fail to do so, they may be liable for deceptive
practices and subject to action by the Federal Trade Commission or
appropriate authority.

end
```
