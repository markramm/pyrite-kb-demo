Date: Fri, 17 Jan 97 17:51 est

To: << various online services and other people >>
Subject: Complaint about fraud via E-mail

Via U.S.Mail:
To:     Postmaster, Huntington Beach, California
To:     U.S. Postal Inspection Service, Chicago, Illinois
To:     Office of the Attorney General, Public Inquiry Unit,
        Sacramento, California

Dear Sir or Madam,

     I write to inform you about a recent electronic break-in
of, I believe, the IBM / Prodigy computer network which enabled
a fraud artist to send a forged letter to me and probably
thousands of other users offering to sell us his secrets for
stealing Internet access, E-mail access, and other similar
techniques.

     I know that most people ignore such letters; I have ignored
many similar ones myself, and I certainly won't answer this one.
But it makes me mad; it is time to fight back.

     To those of you who represent law-enforcement agencies,
please take these break-ins and fraudulent solicitations
seriously.  Millions of American citizens are using the Internet
every day to conduct business, to communicate with friends, and
just to have fun.  We don't accept similar illegal activities
when they are attempted on Main Street, and we must not ignore
them when they are attempted on a cyber street.

     To those of you who are responsible for the security of
your company's Internet connection, please do everything in your
power to tighten up your security to keep "crackers" out.

     To those of you who are innocent victims of this fraud,
perhaps by having your domain name appropriate by the
perpetrator, please join me in raising your voice to complain
about such activity.

     To those of you who write about technology, please help me
shine the clear, bright light of day on the people who steal
machine time, waste human time, and generally try to put one
over on us.  Please also consider explaining how forged E-mail
is created and what system owners and operators can do to fight
it.  Please tell me what industry groups are working to improve
the security of Internet mail and how I can support their
activities.  Many of these illegal schemes depend on the
ignorance of their victims; at least we can end the ignorance!

     I've listed various web sites that help in the analysis of
unsolicited E-mail, and shown how to find the addresses of
various public agencies, in the attached letter.  I hope that by
explaining how to complain about such fraud I can help other
people register their own complaints.

     As a professional software engineer, I'm proud of the
computer networking technology that my colleagues have created.
(I was at MIT in the early 1970s when the folks down the hall
helped invent the ARPAnet; I don't think any of us foresaw just
how extensive it would become).  But I'm ashamed that we haven't
done more to discourage or prevent or even trace the activities
of the few people who prey on ignorance and innocence.  It is
time to act!

     Thank you for your attention.

PG

From: Paul_Green@stratus.com (Paul Green)

I just received a letter yesterday morning (January 16, 1997) from someone
offering to tell me how to obtain free Internet access, free E-mail
remailing, etc., etc., for a mere $29.95.  Sounds way too good to be true.
The whole letter, plus all of the headers, is attached to the end of this
post (some of the headers are presumably fake or forged).

You may or may not get anything of value if you send $29.95 to Mr/Ms "LS"
is Huntington Beach, California.  But you will certainly have $29.95 less
money than when you started, and he/she/it will be that much richer.
Anyone who needs to steal Internet access in order to sell his or her
services isn't going to get my money.

Seems to be that someone is perpetrating a well-hidden scam on the net.  I
believe that the solicitation is probably either fraudulent (you get
nothing in return) or, if you get anything for you money, describes
activities which are themselves illegal (theft of services).  Therefore, I
am not only going to post this letter to the 'net, I'm going to send copies
of this mail (electronically and/or paper) to the various agencies and
companies involved and see if jointly we can find, halt, and punish this
person.

The easiest way to trace the person who is conducting this campaign would
be for the authorities to find out who owns the Post Office box in
Huntington Beach, California!  Maybe the current technology of the Internet
can't prevent determined crackers from stealing service (a sad commentary
on those of us who design and build it), but certainly good old-fashioned
detective work can put a stop to people who break the laws.

I encourage everyone else who receives similar letters to do this same.

Here is what I've learned.

After a search on Dejanews (http://www.dejanews.com/) for 5 or 6 keywords
found in the letter (I forget exactly which ones, but it was something like
"free internet service never pay") I found that this identical letter has
been posted under many (probably forged) names, from many different
Internet access points.  But the text of the letter and the Post Office Box
remains the same.  Interestingly, the payee name (just two letters!)
changes.  It was "SA" in a letter posted by "Bob24@West.net".  It was from
"BillT@Hearst.net" in another letter, also payable to "SA".

I suspect this whole thing is illegal as well, as so I'm sending copies of
the information to the California Attorney General's office, whose home
page I found via the AltaVista query {+State of +California State "Attorney
General"}; http://caag.state.ca.us/.  (Highly recommended; a very
informative and well-organized web site!)

The address given in the web page for general public inquiries is:

     Office of the Attorney General

     P.O. Box 944255
     Sacramento, CA 94244-2550

SUMMARY

This whole thing has cost me (and thus my company) over two hours of my
time yesterday morning!  We've GOT to improve the Internet security,
traceability, logging, etc., to make this kind of thing harder to perform,
easier to trace when it does happen, and so on.

In the meantime, I believe that if we shine the clear light of day on (a)
the people who do this, (b) the companies that let it happen, and (c) on
the weak links in the network technology that facilitate it, we can
gradually bring this problem under control.

Thanks for listening!
