The Australian Government is in the process of co-ordinating the
information services it provides to business, through what it is calling a
National Business Information Service (NBIS).

The Australian NBIS is to be largely web-based, but must also support the
needs of businesses that are not yet web-capable, through such channels as
call-centres and polled-fax.  More information is available from:
		http://www.ausindustry.gov.au/
by going to the Site-Map, and selecting Business Information Service from
within Programs and Services.

NBIS is likely to become part of a wider Single Entry Point (SEP), to
provide businesses with access to all levels of Government.  The SEP is
currently being planned, and the first stage is moving into implementation.
More information about the Single Entry Point for business is at:
http://www.dwrsb.gov.au/smallbus/sept/septhome.htm

I'm a member of a consortium which is conducting a consultancy assignment
for the relevant government agencies, researching into such initiatives.
This email seeks information from members of several relevant Internet
communities.  The remainder of the message provides further details.

BACKGROUND

The kinds of information that the NBIS is focussed on are primarily:
-   compliance obligations, such as licences and registration;
-   assistance programmes, e.g. export facilitation, R&D grants;  and
-   info re tax, industrial relations, occupational health and safety.

The Government's priority concerns include:
-   small and medium enterprises (SMEs);
-   co-ordinated, multi-agency 'windows' or 'single entry points', rather
    than sites maintained by individual agencies;
-   co-ordination across multiple tiers of government (Australia has a
    federal or Commonwealth level, 8 States and Territories, and 700 local
    government areas or Councils);
-   co-ordination with industry associations;
-   co-ordination with, and service to, business intermediaries
    such as accountants, tax agents and business advisors;  and
-   a business life-cycle approach (e.g. a starting-point and coherent
    information for start-up, for annual obligations, for sale, for
    wind-up, etc.).

The intention is to customise the information provision to the needs of the
specific user.  This will require profile information about the user,
however, and is likely to be a medium-term rather than an early feature.

RELEVANT INITIATIVES AROUND THE WORLD

The Australian Government is interested in learning from what other
governments have done, and are doing, in this area.  The 'beacon case
studies' that we're focusing on at the moment are:

-   the Canadian Government's 'Canada Business Service Centres' (CBSC)
    http://www.cbsc.org/

    supported by the key federal department's 'Strategis' site
    http://strategis.ic.gc.ca/engdoc/maintxt.html

    and by the 'Intergovernmental On-Line Information Kiosk'
    http://www.intergov.gc.ca/

-   Washington State's Business Licences Page
    http://www.wa.gov/dol/bpd/buslic.htm

The U.S. Government approach appears to be somewhat diffuse, including:
-   the Department of Commerce's Business Advisor
    http://www.business.gov/
-   the Small Business Administration
    http://www.sbaonline.sba.gov/textonly/

I haven't yet managed to locate any leading examples in Europe.

ASSISTANCE SOUGHT

I would much appreciate assistance in the forms of:

-   suggestions of additional 'beacon case studies' that we should
    examine;

-   reports and articles relevant to the matter;

-   appropriate organisational arrangements, including statutory
    provisions, and contractual arrangements among the parties;

-   key design features of schemes, in particular whether the databases
    are centralised or distributed, and what standards and classification
    schemes have been applied;

* -  how systems have been developed (e.g. who built the system,
  designed the system, defined the data and business rules, and defined
  the data in the system - particularly the extent of government or
  private sector involvement);

-   lessons learnt about system implementation, management, costs, funding
    mechanisms and marketing (including how use of the system is being
    encouraged);

-   take-up statistics, and other performance indicators;  and

-   experiences of and observations on initiatives of this nature.
    Comments from actual businesspeople who've had real experience of
    using such services would be especially valuable.
