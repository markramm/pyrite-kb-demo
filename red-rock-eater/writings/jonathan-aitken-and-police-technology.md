GREAT BRITAIN/SAUDI ARABIA

JONATHAN AITKEN "TWISTS SLOWLY IN THE WIND"

Detectives from the Scotland Yard Specialist Operations
Department are still waiting to interview Jonathan Aitken, the
former Chief Secretary to the Treasury and defence procurement
minister, under investigation concerning allegations of perjury
and conspiring to pervert the course of justice, following the
collapse of his libel action against the "Guardian" newspaper
and Granada Television (INT, n. 63 11).  Mr. Aitken is
currently in the United States, negotiating a book deal about
the affair with a prominent New York publishing house.

Meanwhile, back in Great Britain, friends of Mr. Aitken,
including MPs and journalists in the "conservative, right-wing
media" are suggesting that he lied about his trip to Paris in
September 1993 because he was actually on two secret missions,
one on behalf of the Ministry of Defence (MoD) and the other on
behalf of the Secret Intelligence Service, MI6, with whom he
had a long-standing association.  The scenario also involved a
mistress, whom Mr. Aitken had either taken on his clandestine
missions, or met in Paris, and with whom he had spent the night
at the Hotel Ritz, and an unreliable wife who failed to coverup
for him when asked.  The retired Tory chairman in Mr. Aitken's
former Thanet South constituency, Major John Thomas, supports
the view that "because Mr. Aitken lied does not mean he is
guilty" and claims to have received a letter from the ex-
minister's solicitor, Richard Sykes, saying that his client had
lied about the Paris weekend "for entirely honourable reasons".
Mr. Aitken has told friends that he will return to Great
Britain "after a period of peace and quiet" abroad.

Aitken's libel action the "Guardian" newspaper and Granada
Television came to a sudden and dramatic halt on 20 June, the
13th day  of the hearing, when Aitken's counsel, Charles Gray,
QC, announced that his client no longer wished to pursue the
action.  Under the terms of the settlement, Aitken will pay his
own and 80 percent of defendants' costs, a sum estimated at 2
million.  Aitken's humiliating climbdown, which has certainly
permanently wrecked his political career, was precipitated by
the discovery of new evidence concerning the whereabouts of his
wife and daughter over the weekend of 17-19 September 1993.
The "Guardian" had claimed that Aitken, who was at the time
Minister for Defence Procurement with responsibility for
British arms exports, had spent the period at the Hotel Ritz in
Paris and that his bill had been paid by an Arab business
associate in breach of strict rules on the conduct of ministers
in office.  Aitken denied the claim, insisting that the bill
had been paid by his wife whom he had arranged to meet in
Paris.  The new evidence was comprised of British Airways
records and car hire receipts  which proved that Mrs. Aitken
could not possibly have been in Paris during the weekend in
question.  Aitken was shown to have perjured himself before the
court -- and may now face criminal proceedings as a result --
and was forced to concede the case.

While the British media, along with much of the population, has
enjoyed the spectacle of an arrogant member of the ruling
classes brought low by his own perfidy, serious questions --
not all of which have been satisfactorily answered -- remain
about the background and details of the case.  Chief among
these is why Aitken resorted to such an elaborate and unlikely
story to hide the truth about the payment of the hotel bill,
relying as it did on his wife supposedly organizing what one
reporter called "the most implausible travel arrangements since
Hannibal".

The reason almost certainly lies in the identity of the person
who really did pay:  Prince Mohammed bin Fahd, second son of
Saudi Arabia's King Fahd and now in the running for the top
job.  Aitken had had a long-standing and often controversial
business relationship with the prince and several of his key
aides, especially Said Ayas, an old friend of Aitken (and
godfather to one of his daughters).  Aitken and Ayas were
responsible for the conduct of most Prince Mohammed's extensive
commercial and property interests in Britain, using a variety
of British-registered companies to conduct the business and a
set of companies registered in Panama and Netherlands Antilles
to disguise the ultimate ownership and sources of money (see
"Great Britain - Saudi Al-Yamamah Deal May Be Reexamined" below
in this issue).  Aitken derived considerable personal benefit,
including a 500,000 estate in Kent (owned by a Panama-based
company) and sizable income from his work on the prince's
behalf.

Another offshore outfit owned an Inglewood health farm in
Berkshire, the scene of one of the Guardian/Granada allegations
about Aitken procuring prostitutes for Prince Mohammed and his
entourage.  Stories such as these about Aitken and the Saudis
have been in circulation for some time and one source heard
them about ten years ago from a friend of one of the chaffeurs
of some of the persons involved.

Aitken first met Said Ayas in 1973, but it was not until the
early 1980s that the relationship started to blossom.  The
first public indication of the Aitken-Saudi relationship came
with the exposure in 1988 of a disguised 3 million Saudi
investment in the breakfast TV station TV-AM.  Aitken, who was
managing director of TV-AM's holding company at the time, was
forced out once it was established that he had lied about the
origin of the money -- Prince Mohammed bin Fahd.  At the time
of this setback, however, Aitken had already become a wealthy
man by virtue of his Saudi connection.  Moreover, the sums of
money involved with TV-AM were relatively trivial by comparison
with other contracts -- especially arms deals -- in which
Aitken was involved.

Aitken's political career was on the backburner throughout the
1980s.  First elected as an MP in 1974, he seemed set on the
fast track to high office until he got on the wrong side of
Margaret Thatcher, who was elected Conservative Party leader in
1975 and became prime minister four years later.  This was
widely attributed to Aitken's abortive relationship with
Thatcher's daughter, Carol, but perhaps more important was
Aitken's well-known disdain for her intellectual capabilities
("She probably thinks Sinai is the plural of sinus").  Although
Aitken was consigned to the backbenches for the entire duration
of Thatcher's tenure at Downing Street, his Saudi contacts
proved invaluable when the government was negotiating the Al-
Yamamah rolling series of arms-for-oil deals, worth 15 billion
in the first phase (signed in 1985).

Thatcher was deposed in 1990 and replaced by John Major who had
none of his predecessor's qualms about Aitken.  In 1992, he was
brought into the Cabinet as Minister for Defence Procurement.
The appointment itself was significant inasmuch as the post had
already been promised to  someone else:  the barrister and
veteran Tory MP, Sir Ivan Lawrence.  At the time, Tory
colleagues were extremely surprised by the appointment, and
more than one are convinced that Saudi pressure was
responsible.  Lawrence is also Jewish, as was the Minister of
Defence and Aitken's ministerial boss, Malcolm Rifkind.  The
successor to the original Saudi deal, known as Al-Yamamah 2,
was signed the following year.  Again, substantial
"commissions" were paid on the contracts, among whose
recipients were Mark Thatcher (son of Margaret) and a Briton
known only as "John".  Aitken has denied that he was in any way
involved, although it has been established that his Saudi
patron, Prince  Mohammed, was closely involved in the deal.

The view in London media circles is that Aitken had travelled
to Paris for one of two (and possible both) reasons:  first, to
discuss the division of the estimated 750 million commission
from Al-Yamamah 2;  second, to seek guidance on how to deal
with a request from the Scott Inquiry (INT, n.  n.30 42, 32 46
& 52, 35 50, & 55 12) for information about British-Saudi arms
contracts which were -- according to British intelligence
reports -- known to have been diverted by the Saudis to Iraq.

That question remains open.  What is clear is that Aitken was
prepared to go to considerable lengths to hide his purpose
behind travelling to Paris that weekend.  The way in which he
chose to do so is also somewhat bizarre.  Peter Preston, the
ex-Guardian editor who initiated the investigation, can't
understand why Aitken reacted to their inquiries as he did.
"All [Aitken] had to claim", wrote Preston immediately after
the trial, "was that he'd paid the restaurant bill and Ayas -
quid pro quo - had looked after hotel room:  a natural swap
between friends and not worth a newspaper's time."  Some months
before the case came to court, moreover, Aitken rejected a deal
offered by Guardian/Granada under which both sides would pay
their own costs and withdraw from the action.  Several
colleagues apparently described this decision as "idiotic".

However, once the case came before Justice Popplewell, Aitken's
attitude appeared to have been vindicated.  The
Guardian/Granada side was on the back foot from the beginning
and was forced to withdraw several allegations (which is why
they only got 80% of costs), notably those surrounding Aitken's
knowledge of illicit arms deals to Iran while a director of the
British defence company, BMARC.  The pattern was a familiar
one:  the media side knew that the allegations were true but
were unable to summon enough evidence to convince a judge
adjudicating alone without a jury.  The Paris Ritz story
appeared to be going the same way.  Repeated efforts to dent
Aitken's story during his eight days in the witness box failed.
His version was totally implausible but impossible to disprove.
That was, until Wendy Harris from British Airways Security and
Investigation Branch produced her damning evidence on the 12th
day of the trial.

Aitken's shock upon reading Ms. Harris' affidavit was palpable.
BA Security is very close to British intelligence and Aitken
has long-established links with MI6 (a former senior officer
even gave evidence on his behalf).  British intelligence has
bailed Aitken out on several previous occasions and there was
no reason for him to suppose that they would not do so this
time.

The difference on this occasion was probably the Saudis.
Forthcoming evidence in the case would have been extremely
embarrassing to senior Saudi figures, not least Prince
Mohammed.  Since first making contact with Aitken in the 1970s,
the prince's fortunes have prospered.  Although the second son
of King Fahd, he is acknowledged as his father's favorite and
was, in the early 1990s, granted one of the kingdom's key
domestic posts:  the governorship of the oil-rich Eastern
Province.  With Fahd ailing, a furious power struggle has
unfolded within the house of Saud.  The bad publicity which
might have accrued to Prince Mohammed in the course of the
Aitken case was unacceptable in a clan which abhors almost all
publicity.  Said Ayas, the prince's principal aide, is now
under house arrest, accused of "embezzlement", and being kept
out of public view.  The implication is clear:  MI6 and the
Saudis had decided to dump Aitken following the loss of his
parliamentary seat in the Mayday electoral massacre.  With no
pressing reason to sustain him, the protection of the British-
Saudi relationship -- as Aitken would be the first to recognize
-- was paramount.

Over the years, Aitken has devoted some of his energies to
writing books.  These include a "revisionist" biography of
former U.S. president, Richard Nixon, published in 1994.  It is
thus deeply ironic that Aitken should not have learnt the
lesson of his subject's career:  that a coverup is often more
dangerous to its architect than the original action.  Aitken
will surely recognize the condition in which he has now been
left.  He will recognize John Erlichman's famous Watergate
dismissal of the hapless FBI director, Pat Gray:  "let him hang
there; let him twist slowly, slowly in the wind."

TECHNOLOGY AND TECHNIQUES

Intelligence, N. 64, 14 July 1997, p. 2

HIGH-TECH CYBER COP NOW OUT ON THE BEAT

In Texas and Vancouver, computer-based law enforcement tools
have been locally developed, are being tested, and are on their
way to a promising future.  The Texas Transportation Institute,
specialized in traffic-flow studies and guardrails or street
signals designs, is looking for backing from high-tech
companies to put Advanced Law Enforcement Response Technology
(Alert) on the market.  The police patrol car-borne computer
system, developed with Texas Agriculture and Military (A&M)
University in College Station, was recently unveiled,
integrated into two prototype vehicles which are being tested
"on the beat".  The Institute and the University need $7
million over the next two years to fully develop and test
Alert, and try to get it on the market by the end of 1999.  The
project is overseen by Bill Baker for the Federal Highway
Administration which paid $1.7 million for initial research as
part of a 1994 project to find a way to speed up the transfer
of accident information to the Department of Transportation.

Alert project manager, Sandra Parsons, says the system
consolidates all police patrol car functions -- radio, lights,
sirens and radar -- and acts as a rapid data link and data-
storage device for such basic operations as filing accident
reports and watching for wanted criminals and missing children.
By swiping a Texas driver's license through the system's bar-
code reader, Alert automatically fills out requested forms, and
additional information, including a driver's signature, can be
added with an electronic pen on a hand-held computer pad linked
to the system.  In early June, the Smithsonian Institution
recognized Alert for its technological accomplishments, and
several federal agencies, including the FBI and Secret Service,
have expressed interest in the system.

"North of the border", in Vancouver, geographic information
system (GIS) data and direct marketing have been linked by
computer to produce Orion, a new law-enforcement tool.  This
locally-developed geographic "profiling" system enables police
to analyze vast quantities of information -- psychological
profiles, aerial photos, postal codes, motor vehicle licensing
information, letters from criminals, census data and land-use
records -- to find out where a serial criminal is most likely
to live.  The new Vancouver Police Department Geographic
Profiling Section, headed by Detective Inspector Kim Rossmo,
helped develop the Orion system which manages "mail-outs" to
the targeted areas with requests for further information and
descriptions of wanted suspects.  According to Rossmo, "We've
even been successful getting mail-outs into the offender's
home, with interesting results."

Orion software performs a spatial analysis of the furnished
data and produces a three-dimensional "jeopardy surface" which
is a multicolored map highlighting the most probable target
areas.  It "gives you an optimal searching path for the area,"
according to Rossmo who developed a prototype of Orion after
receiving a doctorate at Simon Fraser University where
environmental criminologists Paul and Patricia Brantingham had
developed a model showing that where a criminal lives affects
where he is likely to commit a crime.  Rossmo worked the other
way around, trying to show that "most offenders commit crimes
in their `comfort zone,' which is often not far from where they
live", the conceptual basis for Orion whose "jeopardy surface"
highlights the probable "comfort zone" of an individual
criminal.

Orion is being marketed by Environmental Criminology Research
in Vancouver and the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) has
already signed a $225,000 contract to buy the system which has
the tremendous advantage of being compatible with the RCMP's
Violent Crime Linkage Analysis System (ViCLAS) database which
links crimes committed over a period of time or in apparently
unrelated locations.  The Ontario Provincial Police is
supposedly "very interested" in Orion, and Rossmo is reportedly
swamped with requests from other police forces.  He reportedly
helped British police and New York City police to successfully
investigate series of rapes.  But Rossmo can't handle all the
requests that come in, since each case requires about two weeks
work, but other Canadian specialists are now being trained to
use Orion.
