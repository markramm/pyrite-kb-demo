Information Studies 289 -- Interpreting Documents

Fall 2005

Document discussion assignment.

During the middle portion of each class meeting (weeks 2 through 10), we will
analyze documents that students have brought in. We will determine the time
available for discussion simply by dividing the total discussion time across
the nine weeks by the number of students who sign up for the class. Please
consult the course readings to guess which weeks would be best for discussing
your particular documents, based on the types of topics that are likely
to apply to them. Although it would be ideal for your documents to relate
directly to the previous week's readings, it is not crucial. You do not need
to bring in the original documents; photocopies are fine. Bring in enough
photocopies that everyone in the class can refer to a copy during the
discussion. It does not matter if the documents are in English. If they are
in English then everyone can read them, but documents that are not in English
might bring a greater diversity of issues into the discussion.

Documents for our purposes include any sort of written words, including
letters, manuscripts, pamphlets, and books. If the document has more than a
few pages, bring only a few well-chosen pages to class. In choosing documents
to bring in, there are two important issues. First, you should choose
documents that you believe are interesting and important. The second issue
is your access to information about the documents and the settings in which
they were made and used. Please spend the first ten minutes of the discussion
time outlining this information as a basis for discussion. Important types
of information about the documents include:

* who wrote them and how* what they mean (particularly if they are not in English)* diverse aspects of the language in which they were written* their internal organization* whether they were typical or atypical examples of something* their metaphors and other rhetorical devices* what if anything was new about them, and what was routine* their relationships to other documents (e.g., revisions, rebuttals, etc)* their authors' biographies* the technology of their manufacture* who read them* the activities within which they read them* the relationships between authors and readers* the institutions within which the authors and readers were operating* aspects of their meaning that would have been important to their authors and
                              readers but that will not be evident to us* what their authors were trying to accomplish* and their readers likewise* whether they are authentic and what that even means* what other types of assessments of quality people in that setting would have
                                      made* anything about that setting that is alien to our experience but would have
                                        been important to the uses people made of them* the people, places, things, and ideas that they were about* what kind of overt or tacit agenda the author had in representing any of
                                            that stuff in particular ways* the history of the uses that various types of people (e.g., scholars) have
                                              made of the document since it was first produced* how the document eventually became a topic of analysis for us
