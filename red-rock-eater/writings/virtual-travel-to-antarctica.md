support crew who made the difficult decision to "Winter Over" and their
reflections as they watch the others leave.  There are only 28 people left
at the South Pole and it is closed for the dark Winter months.  If you want
to know what it is like to live in that world you can follow the thoughts
and reflections of these Antarctic adventurers through their journals.

Ever dreamed of about taking your students on a trip to Antarctica...
or getting them engaged with scientists exploring new frontiers?

Maybe the cold, the distance or the cost made this seem like a
no more than a dream.  But technology continues to find ways to
shape our reality.

A multimedia learning package can be assembled for almost no cost
... and you can start right here on the Net!

The Passport to Knowledge series has made available a number of
components:

* Videotapes* Printed Teacher's Guides (published by Prentice Hall School)* A collections of online Resource Materials* "Field Journals" written by researchers in the Antarctica,* A set of student questions asked by students all across America, and
          their answers back from "the Ice"* Opportunities for student and teacher interaction - online* Offline resources

---->>> V I D E O T A P E S

There are four one-hour videos, full of information about Antarctica,
interactions between scientists and students, and examples of classrooms
activities that can help your students get involved in their own hands-on
research. Originally broadcast on many public television stations, they
can also be see on NASA-TV. Educators have extended off-air record and
in-class rights!

Watch NASA-TV on Saturday Feb 25, they will run back to back at
08:00 thru 12:00. NASA-TV can be found on SPACENET 2, transponder 5
(channel 9) frequency 3880.0 Mhz, horizontal polarization; the audio
can be found on 6.2 and 6.8. Many cable systems, community colleges
and other local satellite systems are used to accessing NASA-TV, and
may be willing to make tapes for you, if you so arrange.

Videotapes may be ordered at cost from NASA CORE, 216-774-1051 ex 293-294

 ----->>>>>>  R E S O U R C E   M A T E R I A L S

The Internet is a vast library of exciting materials.  We have helped create
a focal point for information about Antarctica and research topics that are
related to this vast and exciting continent.  Our World Wide Web page and
gopher links will provide an instant library of rich and varied resources
for your students.

A special set of NSF files allow access to materials previously never
publicly available, such as the Field Manual and the "Your Stay" series
provided to every researcher who visits Antarctica, designed to keep
them safe in the extremes of weather. Have your students research what
it takes to stay safe in a crevasse field, or how to build a snow shelter!
Let them read the brochure which describes everyday life at the
Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station!

 ----->>>>  F I E L D    J O U R N A L S

Your students can follow along with some of the different travelers
who go to the ICE to as researchers, members of the support
teams, or even tv producers!

Your students can imagine they have joined 17-year old Elizabeth Felton
as she follows her dream of later becoming and astronaut by going to
the South Pole to study astronomy.  Or maybe some of your students will
want to be film makers and will enjoy the journey from the perspectives of
the producers of Live from Antarctica.  Some may be captivated by how
April Lloyd, a 3rd-grade teacher from Virginia, explores this new world
in the hope of bringing back ideas to help make her teaching more exciting.

Or perhaps it will be the researcher exploring the ozone hole or the
radio operator who use her skill to make research safe that will
provide the perspective that you are looking for.  Teachers who have
participated report that often even reserved students have become engaged
by their encounters with real researchers reporting from real locations.

You'll find simple instructions online.

LIVE FROM THE STATOSPHERE will involve several science museums and
other partners, who will host overnight "camp-ins" in conjunction
with live programming from flight aboard NASA's Kuiper Airborne
Observatory, with its infrared telescope. We hope the project will
begin with the opening of the 1995 school year in September,
with -- perhaps -- two programs airing during school hours, and
then a "virtual night flight" aboard the Kuiper. The Kuiper's
infrared telescope will let students look at planets and stars and
galaxies...and at the origins of celestial objects.

LIVE FROM THE HUBBLE SPACE TELESCOPE will provide us a unique opportunity
to experiment with the nature of our partnership. In September while
we are taking you on the Kuiper, we will announce a kind of "Request
for Student Proposals" for astronomical observations to be undertaken
ACTUALLY ON THE HUBBLE SPACE TELESCOPE! An agreement with the Space
Telescope Science Institute, which manages Hubble for NASA, will allow
some students to undertake investigations on the nation's premier space
telescope, through the commitment of Director's Discretionary Observing
Time!!!  Developing a quality proposal will obviously require a large
amount of background information, but fortunately the STScI Home Page
and STEIS is already very extensive, with excellent data and pictures!
Check it out at:

http://stsci.edu

or:

ftp stsci.edu
user name, anonymous
password, enter last name or user id

We plan to have astronomers, and graduate and undergraduate students,
"tele-mentor" students around the nation over the Internet, and then
select winning proposals, and refine them, with the observations
themselves forming the focus of LIVE FROM THE HUBBLE SPACE TELESCOPE
in late Spring/early Summer 1996. This process, also involving "peer
review" of proposals by student evaluators, is designed to mirror how
NSF and other research agencies actually select proposals.
