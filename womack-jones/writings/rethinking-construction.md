The 1998 UK government report known as the "Egan Report," formally titled "Rethinking Construction: The Report of the Construction Task Force." Commissioned in October 1997 by Deputy Prime Minister John Prescott and chaired by Sir John Egan (former chief executive of Jaguar Cars), the report applied lean thinking principles to the UK construction industry. [[daniel-t-jones]] contributed lean thinking expertise to the report. His LEI biography has variously described his role as advising the UK government on the report; the exact nature of his involvement (task force member, advisor, or contributor to specific sections) has not been confirmed from primary sources and should not be assumed to be primary authorship.

## Content and Impact

The report promoted wider use of lean construction methods, arguing for less waste, shorter timelines, and cost savings by applying principles that had proven successful in manufacturing. Together with the earlier Latham Report ("Constructing the Team," 1994), it drove efficiency improvements in UK construction industry practice during the early 2000s.

The report led to the creation of several industry bodies: the Construction Best Practice Programme, the Movement for Innovation, and influenced the activities of the Construction Industry Board and Design Build Foundation.

## Lean Application to Construction

The Egan Report represents one of the earliest systematic attempts to translate the [[lean-enterprise]] concept into a non-manufacturing context. The construction industry's characteristics — project-based work, fragmented supply chains, adversarial contracting, and high waste rates — made it both a promising and challenging target for lean thinking.

The report's lean-influenced recommendations included:
- Committed leadership and a focus on the customer
- Integrated project teams replacing adversarial contracting
- Quality-driven agenda with zero-defect targets
- Commitment to people (training, health, safety)

## Reception and Critique

In May 2008, ten years after publication, Sir John Egan assessed the results as "pretty patchy," giving the industry about four out of ten marks — suggesting that the translation of lean principles to construction faced significant implementation challenges.

## Relationship to the KB

This report is significant in the [[lean-expansion-era]] as evidence of Jones's role extending lean beyond manufacturing through institutional channels (UK government policy). It demonstrates both the ambition of lean's portability claims and the difficulty of translating manufacturing-originated frameworks to fundamentally different industries.
