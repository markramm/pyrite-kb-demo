The 1996 book that codified the [[five-lean-principles]] into the portable framework that defined a generation of management practice. Authored by [[james-p-womack]] and [[daniel-t-jones]], it was revised and updated in 2003. The book won the [[shingo-institute]] Prize and is the companion volume to [[machine-that-changed-the-world]] — where that book described what lean plants look like, this one prescribes how to create them.

## The five principles as translation device

The book's central intellectual contribution is the articulation of five principles that abstract TPS into a management framework applicable beyond automotive manufacturing:

1. **Value** — define value from the customer's perspective
2. **Value Stream** — identify the value stream for each product and eliminate waste
3. **Flow** — make value-creating steps flow without interruption
4. **Pull** — let the customer pull value from the producer rather than pushing
5. **Perfection** — pursue perfection through continuous improvement

See [[five-lean-principles]] for the full concept entry. These principles are an abstraction from TPS, not a description of it — the five-principles framework emphasizes the logical structure of lean rather than the operational mechanisms (kanban, andon, heijunka, jidoka) that Toyota actually uses. This is documented in the [[five-principles-as-abstraction]] note.

## What TPS concepts are being translated

The five principles translate specific TPS mechanisms:

- **Value/Value Stream** — adapts the TPS emphasis on customer-takt alignment and muda (waste) identification, drawing on [[taiichi-ohno]]'s seven wastes
- **Flow** — translates continuous-flow manufacturing and the TPS opposition to batch-and-queue production
- **Pull** — names and abstracts the kanban system
- **Perfection** — translates kaizen (continuous improvement) as a philosophical orientation toward an ideal state

The translation is deliberate: Womack and Jones wanted a framework that could be taught to managers who would never visit a Toyota plant. The five principles became the lean movement's primary teaching scaffold.

## Target audience and scope expansion

Unlike [[machine-that-changed-the-world]], which addressed automotive executives, this book targets managers across industries. The case studies range from automotive (Toyota) to consumer goods to wire management ([[art-byrne]]'s Wiremold transformation) to healthcare to retail. This scope expansion was both the book's strength (lean is general, not industry-specific) and a source of later critique: applying manufacturing flow concepts to knowledge work requires further translation steps that this book does not fully develop.

## The lean enterprise concept

The book introduces the [[lean-enterprise]] framing — the idea that lean transformation must extend across the entire value chain, not just individual production facilities. This sets up the agenda of [[lean-enterprise-institute]], which Womack founded in 1997 (see [[lei-founding]]).

## Revised 2003 edition

The 2003 revision added a new preface and updated case studies. It appeared in the same year as the Toyota-inspired boom in lean adoption across industries, cementing this book's status as the primary reference text for lean practitioners outside manufacturing.

## Downstream transmission

The five-principles framework became the conceptual foundation for lean's expansion beyond manufacturing. The Poppendiecks' lean software development work explicitly cites this book; the Lean Startup movement treats these principles as background assumptions. The [[lean-transmission-chain]] note traces how each principle was reinterpreted in subsequent domains. The book's publication is marked as [[lean-thinking-publication]] in the KB's event timeline.
