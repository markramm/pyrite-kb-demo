Reinertsen's first book, co-authored with [[preston-smith]]. Aimed at product development managers, it introduced the core argument that speed-to-market is an economic decision variable, not just a project management concern. Made the case that overlapping activities, reducing [[batch-size-reduction|batch sizes]], and accelerating [[fast-feedback|feedback loops]] could dramatically reduce time-to-market without sacrificing quality.

Less theoretically rigorous than his later works but more accessible to a practitioner audience. The book reframed time-to-market from a scheduling problem into an economic one — the foundational move that Reinertsen would deepen and formalize across the next two decades. Established the framework that [[managing-the-design-factory]] would subsequently extend with queueing theory and mathematical grounding.

The book's significance lies less in its specific tools than in its framing: by insisting that development speed has quantifiable economic consequences, Reinertsen laid the groundwork for [[economic-framework-for-prioritization|economic approaches to product development decisions]]. This positioned the work as an early precursor to what would eventually become the lean software and agile movements' economic justifications. Its publication is documented in the event [[publication-developing-products-half-time]].
