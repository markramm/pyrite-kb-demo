"The Fifth Discipline Fieldbook," published in 1994 by [[doubleday-currency]], was a direct response to the reception of [[fifth-discipline-1990]]. Readers of the original book had found the framework compelling but were uncertain how to apply it in practice. The Fieldbook, co-authored by [[peter-senge]] with [[art-kleiner]], [[charlotte-roberts]], [[richard-ross]], and [[bryan-smith]], supplies what the original deliberately withheld: 593 pages of exercises, tools, case studies, and practical guidance for building [[learning-organization]] capacity across all [[five-disciplines]].

The structure of the Fieldbook mirrors the five disciplines, with sections organized around each practice. The mental models section includes exercises for surfacing assumptions, techniques drawn from [[chris-argyris]] and [[donald-schon]]'s action science — particularly the "left-hand column" exercise — and guidance on conducting productive inquiry. The team learning section develops tools for [[dialogue-practice]], distinguishing dialogue from discussion and offering facilitation methods rooted in [[david-bohm]]'s work. The systems thinking section provides accessible introductions to [[causal-loop-diagrams]] and the [[systems-archetypes]], with worked examples from real organizational contexts.

The Fieldbook introduced the concept of "the SoL community" before the [[society-for-organizational-learning]] was formally founded, treating the book itself as a community artifact — each section draws on contributions from practitioners working in diverse organizational settings. This editorial approach, pioneered by [[art-kleiner]] and the co-authorship team, became the template for subsequent volumes in the series: [[dance-of-change-1999]] and [[schools-that-learn-2000]]. The multi-voice format reflects a deliberate epistemology: no single author or organization holds the answer to building learning organizations.

The Fieldbook's influence on organizational practice has arguably exceeded that of the original book for working practitioners. Consultants, HR professionals, and managers who found [[fifth-discipline-1990]] inspiring but abstract found in the Fieldbook a direct path to application. The book's treatment of [[mental-models]] work — particularly the distinction between espoused theory and theory-in-use — brought Argyris's rigorous but demanding framework into mainstream organizational development in an accessible form. The [[causal-loop-diagrams]] section has been used as a teaching resource in systems thinking workshops for three decades.
