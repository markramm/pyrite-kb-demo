*Adrenaline Junkies and Template Zombies: Understanding Patterns of Project Behavior* (2008), published by [[dorset-house-publishing]], is the most expansive collaborative work of the [[atlantic-systems-guild]], co-authored by DeMarco, [[timothy-lister]], [[peter-hruschka]], [[steve-mcmenamin]], [[james-robertson]], and [[suzanne-robertson]]. It is an organizational behavior pattern catalog: 86 named patterns describing recurring dynamics in software projects, presented as observations rather than prescriptions.

## The pattern catalog format

The book's format is deliberately non-prescriptive. Unlike most project management writing, which tells readers what to do, *Adrenaline Junkies* documents what actually happens in software organizations — the dysfunctional patterns as well as the productive ones. Each pattern has a name (the titles are memorably vivid), a description of the behavior, and an analysis of its organizational causes and consequences.

The patterns range across the full life of a project: planning dysfunctions (the "template zombie" who fills in process artifacts without understanding their purpose), execution dynamics (the "adrenaline junkie" manager who creates crises to feel needed), team behaviors, management interventions, and organizational responses to failure.

## Relationship to the Atlantic Systems Guild tradition

The book represents the collective observational intelligence of six consultants who had spent decades working with software organizations across multiple industries and cultures. The pattern format was a way of organizing this accumulated experience into a transferable form. The [[atlantic-systems-guild]]'s consulting practice had given its members access to organizational dysfunction at close range; the patterns are essentially a taxonomy of what they had seen.

The underlying theoretical commitments are continuous with [[peopleware]]: the [[peopleware-thesis]] that organizational and sociological factors dominate technical ones, the importance of [[team-jell]] and how management behavior can prevent it, the [[spanish-theory-of-management]]'s destructive effects on project culture. What the pattern format adds is granularity — rather than arguing for the thesis at a high level, the book documents the specific mechanisms by which organizational dysfunction manifests in recognizable behavioral episodes.

## The "adrenaline junkie" and "template zombie" archetypes

The two title patterns are the most widely cited and capture the book's central diagnosis. The adrenaline junkie is the manager who confuses crisis response with competence — who creates urgency not because the work requires it but because urgency feels like leadership. This pattern is a direct instantiation of the [[spanish-theory-of-management]]: the belief that pressure is the primary management tool, applied at the individual personality level.

The template zombie is the complement: the organization that substitutes process compliance for thought, filling in mandatory artifacts without connecting them to actual work. The zombie pattern captures the failure mode of process-heavy organizations — the [[organizational-learning-disability]] expressed as bureaucratic ritual rather than genuine practice.

## Position in the bibliography

*Adrenaline Junkies* is the last major collaborative work in the DeMarco-Lister-Guild tradition and represents its most mature observational form. The book received the [[jolt-award-adrenaline-junkies|Jolt Award]] for best general computing book in 2009, the third Jolt Award for the Guild. The book is more descriptive than prescriptive — a catalog of what to recognize rather than a recipe for what to do. This reflects both the accumulated wisdom of the Guild members and a certain pragmatic modesty: by 2008, the pattern catalog format (familiar from the software design patterns tradition) offered a way to transmit organizational knowledge without claiming more systematicity than the messy reality of organizations supports.
